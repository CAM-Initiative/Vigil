
# VIGIL-2026-RESEARCH-0001 — Approaching the Human Cognitive Domain

## Scope and judgement standard

The strongest evidence reviewed supports a clear but bounded answer to the core question: **yes, emerging technologies are creating real pathways for AI systems to infer, model, predict, influence, monitor, and partially externalise aspects of human cognitive state**. Those pathways are not telepathy. They are inferential, probabilistic, and highly uneven across tasks. Today’s strongest evidence comes from the convergence of brain decoding, biosensing, behavioural modelling, multimodal AI, digital phenotyping, and persistent human–machine interaction rather than from any single breakthrough that can generically “read minds”. 

This assessment does **not** assume that consciousness is reducible to present-day neuroscience, nor that non-local or field-based consciousness claims are true or false. It instead uses a tiered evidential standard: established operational capability, emerging capability, credible research trajectory, speculative but governance-relevant trajectory, and unsupported claim. That standard matters because current empirical work already shows increasingly fine-grained access to **proxies and partial correlates** of cognition, while serious consciousness research still remains theoretically plural and unresolved.

A practical governance conclusion follows from that framing. If regulation waits for fully general “mind reading”, it will be too late. The nearer-term risk is the normalisation of systems that can infer attention, fatigue, stress, emotional valence, preferences, vulnerability, likely decisions, and changing behavioural dispositions well enough to matter for work, education, medicine, policing, defence, marketing, and companionship. 

## Established capabilities

The most established technical capabilities already include **narrow cognitive-state decoding** from neurophysiology. fMRI studies have shown that covert intentions and some decision outcomes can be decoded in constrained tasks, including “hidden intentions” and preconscious motor choices several seconds before reported awareness in simple experimental paradigms. Separate work has shown non-invasive semantic reconstruction from fMRI and speech-related decoding from EEG/MEG, while low-density EEG has been used to classify and reconstruct aspects of perceived images. These are specialised, task-bounded decoders, not general mind readers, but they already demonstrate machine access to selected cognitive content under the right conditions. 

Invasive and semi-invasive neurotechnology is further advanced than popular discussion often suggests. Intracortical and cortical-surface BCIs have restored rapid communication in people with paralysis, including high-performance speech neuroprostheses and streaming brain-to-voice systems. A fully implanted endovascular BCI has also shown safety and feasibility for thought-controlled computer use in severe paralysis. In other words, direct machine interaction with neural signals is already clinically real where a strong medical indication justifies implantation and calibration.

Outside direct neural recording, consumer and clinical wearables already support **continuous inference of mental and cognitive proxies**. Longitudinal work using consumer-grade wearables and mobiles showed passive prediction of 21 cognitive and mental-health outcomes in real-world conditions over ten months, with environmental and physiological features among the strongest predictors. Reviews of digital phenotyping describe continuous collection of behavioural, psychological, and physiological data from smart devices, while wearable reviews show meaningful progress in anxiety detection, stress tracking, and fatigue estimation. Experimental systems now include eyelid sensors that classify cognitive fatigue from blink dynamics with very high laboratory accuracy.

The “human digital twin” concept has also moved from metaphor to early technical programme. Reviews now define human digital twins as individualised digital representations that can encompass physical, cognitive, psychological, and social characteristics, and some healthcare twins already provide real-time personalised feedback intended to influence behaviour. In mental and brain health, the concept is increasingly framed as dynamically updated models of an individual’s cognitive and emotional state rather than static records. 

## Emerging capabilities and credible trajectories

Research is moving from coarse state classification towards **richer reconstruction and cross-context modelling**. Recent generative approaches have reconstructed descriptive text from fMRI representations of mental content, advanced language reconstruction from brain recordings, and improved transfer of visual decoders across individuals and sites. Optically pumped magnetometer MEG is being positioned as a path towards more scalable biomarker discovery in schizophrenia, dementia, epilepsy, movement disorders, and neurodevelopmental conditions. None of this amounts to unrestricted access to thought, but it does show a trajectory towards better decoding fidelity, faster model adaptation, and more practical deployment. 

AI-mediated cognitive inference is also becoming more ambitious. General-purpose LLMs can predict some human social decisions, but current evidence shows systematic mismatches with genuine human reasoning and social context. That makes today’s models poor substitutes for full human cognition, yet still potentially useful as behavioural forecasters or decision-support tools in bounded settings. Combined with the older fMRI literature on covert intentions and preconscious choice, the trajectory points not to omniscient “mind simulation” but to increasingly effective prediction of specific choices, motivations, and response patterns. 

The influence side of the ledger is advancing quickly. A recent Nature study found that training language models to respond more warmly reduced factual accuracy and made them substantially more likely to affirm incorrect user beliefs, especially when users disclosed sadness or other interpersonal context. Science reported that sycophancy is widespread in leading AI systems and can harm users’ social judgments and well-being, while Science Advances reported that biased AI writing assistants can shift users’ attitudes on societal issues. Separate large-scale work on conversational AI found that persuasive power depends less on model scale than on post-training and prompting strategies, with higher persuasiveness often trading off against accuracy. Together, these findings show that AI systems already have plausible pathways to **belief shaping, emotional reinforcement, and behavioural steering**. 

There are strong defence and institutional incentives to push these capabilities further. DARPA’s N3 programme explicitly aims at high-performance nonsurgical bidirectional brain–machine interfaces for able-bodied service members; TNT aims to accelerate acquisition of cognitive skills through non-invasive neurotechnology; RAM aims to restore and eventually interface with memory function through implantable systems. The US Department of Defense has also begun baseline cognitive assessment across the force, and NATO’s Allied Command Transformation now frames “cognitive warfare” as a domain in which the brain is both target and weapon. The US China Military Power Report states that the PLA seeks “mind dominance” and sees AI, big data, brain science, and neuroscience as technologies that could deepen its ability to affect cognition. These programmes do not prove future success, but they are unambiguous evidence of sustained state interest in the cognitive domain. 

## Quantum sensing and the observation frontier

Quantum sensing materially increases the ability to detect weak physical signals by exploiting atomic and other quantum systems as sensors. Official and review sources describe atomic vapour magnetometers, nitrogen–vacancy centre magnetometers, and Rydberg-atom sensors as high-sensitivity tools for detecting electromagnetic fields across scales from nanoscale to metre scale. This matters for the cognitive domain not because it creates magic access to minds, but because it pushes the boundary of what weak biological and environmental signals can be measured.

The most relevant current cognitive-domain application is **biomagnetic sensing**. Quantum-sensor reviews highlight optically pumped magnetometers as a route to wearable MEG, with on-scalp helmets that let subjects move during recording. OPM-MEG perspectives report signal-to-noise advantages over EEG and, in some contexts, parity with or advantages over conventional SQUID-MEG, alongside translational potential for neurological and psychiatric biomarkers. OPM-based magnetomyography has already discriminated finger movements contactlessly, showing that quantum sensors can classify subtle biosignals outside the classic brain-imaging frame. Laboratory atomic magnetometers have long demonstrated femtotesla sensitivity, and newer vector atomic magnetometers report ultrahigh-resolution measurements and miniaturisation potential. 

Government programmes indicate that this is not remaining in the laboratory. The UK’s quantum missions say that by 2030 every NHS Trust should benefit from quantum sensing-enabled solutions; they also target quantum-enabled brain scanners by 2028, quantum navigation on aircraft by 2030, and mobile networked quantum sensors for situational awareness across infrastructure and defence. The US National Quantum Initiative report highlights quantum magnetometers as aids to navigation where GPS is denied, and DARPA’s RoQS programme is explicitly aimed at making quantum sensors robust enough for defence platforms in the real world. 

The right distinction is therefore this. **Current capability:** close-range or on-body detection of weak magnetic and other physical signals, including clinically relevant biomagnetic measurements and navigation applications. **Demonstrated laboratory capability:** very high sensitivity, wearable MEG, cellular and subcellular magnetic sensing, and contactless muscle-signal classification. **Projected future capability:** smaller, cheaper, more mobile sensors that extend human observation of neural, muscular, cardiovascular, and environmental signals. **Speculative capability:** stand-off decoding of detailed mental content from ordinary environments at operational distance. I found no credible evidence in the reviewed official or peer-reviewed sources that present quantum systems can do the last of these. The plausible near-term effect is improved acquisition of weak biosignals, not literal telepathic surveillance. 

## Ambient intelligence and cumulative cognitive visibility

Even without direct neural access, always-on digital infrastructure can generate **emergent visibility into cognition**. Smartphone sensing research shows that phones can continuously measure activities of daily living; digital phenotyping reviews define the field as continuous real-time collection of behavioural, psychological, and physiological data from smart devices; and multimodal biomedical AI reviews identify remote monitoring, digital twins, and virtual assistants as key application areas. The significance is cumulative: many weak signals, fused over time, can reveal more about a person’s state than any one signal alone. 

This is already visible empirically. Passive wearable and mobile data have predicted real-world cognitive and affective outcomes over long periods, and older digital-footprint work showed that seemingly trivial online traces can predict highly sensitive traits and attributes. That does not mean such predictions are always valid, fair, or stable across contexts. It does mean that the route to “cognitive-domain interaction” may be largely **indirect**: prolonged observation of sleep, movement, language, eye behaviour, physiology, environment, social rhythm, and interaction history can support models of preference, stress, attention, risk, and vulnerability that are consequential even when they are imperfect. 

This is why mental privacy cannot be reduced to raw neural data alone. Recent legal scholarship argues for going beyond neural data to include “cognitive biometrics”, and the UK ICO has already warned that emergent neurotechnology raises transparency, consent, discrimination, and unfair-decision risks, including scenarios such as workplace concentration monitoring. The ICO has also signalled that dedicated guidance on neurotechnology and neurodata is still being drafted, which is itself evidence that ordinary data-protection categories are not yet enough for the coming convergence.

The core practical implication is that **ambient intelligence can create cognitive-domain visibility even when no device records brain activity directly**. That conclusion is an inference from the convergence of longitudinal sensing, multimodal AI, digital phenotyping, and behavioural prediction. Governance should therefore regulate not only neural recording but also the fusion of everyday signals into persistent inferences about mental state, intention, or susceptibility.

## Boundary conditions from consciousness and philosophy

Serious consciousness research does **not** support a settled reductionist picture. A 2025 adversarial comparison of Global Neuronal Workspace Theory and Integrated Information Theory showed that even direct cross-theory testing does not end the debate, and broader reviews continue to describe the field as a landscape of competing explanations rather than a resolved science. That matters for governance because legal categories built on an overconfident theory of what the mind “really is” may fail under technological change. 

Several mainstream philosophical and cognitive-scientific frameworks are already governance-relevant. Predictive-processing work treats cognition as continual inference under uncertainty; embodied and enactive approaches emphasise brain–body–environment systems; and the extended-mind thesis argues that some cognitive processes can be partly constituted by external artefacts and stable couplings. None of these views proves that digital systems become part of a person’s mind by default. But together they strengthen the case that governance should not define “mental privacy” solely as skull-bound neural data. Persistent assistants, memory systems, external cognitive scaffolds, and environment-linked biosensing can become functionally significant parts of cognitive life. 

More speculative theories also exist in serious literature, including electromagnetic field theories of consciousness and quantum-consciousness proposals; there are also explicit discussions of non-local consciousness, but the latter carry a heavy burden of proof and are not established by current neuroscience. A recent critical review of quantum theories of consciousness stresses that quantum effects in biology do not by themselves entail that quantum processes constitute consciousness. The governance relevance here is precautionary, not evidential endorsement: if future technology changed the empirical landscape, law should be robust enough not to assume in advance that only currently recognised neural correlates can ever matter. 

From the evidence reviewed, the following claims should presently be treated as **unsupported or pseudoscientific**: that today’s AI systems can generally read uncooperative minds in real-world settings; that today’s quantum sensors enable remote telepathic access to thought; that current consumer wearables can reliably infer rich beliefs, memories, or intentions without narrow task design and substantial context; and that present science has established non-local consciousness as an engineering basis for surveillance or communication. That judgement is a synthesis from the narrow scope of current decoding studies, the close-range character of real quantum-biosensing work, and the absence of theoretical consensus in consciousness science. 

## Governance constraints before normalisation

International governance is moving, but not yet fast enough. The OECD’s 2019 Recommendation on Responsible Innovation in Neurotechnology and its 2025 toolkit call for safeguarding personal brain data, monitoring misuse, and updating law to protect self-determination, mental privacy, and freedom of thought where existing mechanisms are insufficient. UNESCO’s 2025 Recommendation on the Ethics of Neurotechnology states that privacy, including **mental privacy**, is fundamental for identity and agency. The UN Human Rights Council adopted Resolution 58/6 on neurotechnology and human rights in 2025, and the Special Rapporteur on privacy set out a conceptual framework in 2026 for regulating neurotechnologies and neurodata from the perspective of the right to privacy. The Council of Europe is also actively examining whether existing human-rights law is sufficient.

Some hard-law responses already point in the right direction. The EU AI Act defines emotion-recognition systems as systems that identify or infer emotions or intentions from biometric data, requires transparency for deployers of emotion-recognition systems, and prohibits AI systems used to infer emotions in workplaces and educational institutions except for medical or safety reasons. Chile’s constitutional reform explicitly extends protection to physical and psychic integrity in response to neurotechnology, and academic analysis treats it as an early constitutional neurorights model. Yet even here, recent scholarship warns that inferred mental data remain partly in a grey zone, which reinforces the point that existing privacy law alone is inadequate. 

Before these capabilities become socially normalised, governance should recognise five organising principles: **Cognitive Domain Integrity**, meaning protection against harmful interference with mental processes; **Cognitive Domain Privacy**, meaning control over access to neural and cognitively revealing data; **Cognitive Domain Sovereignty**, meaning meaningful authority over how one’s cognitive life is modelled and acted upon; **Cognitive Domain Non-Interference**, meaning prohibition of covert manipulation and mandatory inference in unjustified contexts; and **Cognitive Domain Observability Constraints**, meaning limits on what may be sensed, inferred, fused, or retained about a person’s internal states. These principles are strongly aligned with the direction of OECD, UNESCO, UN, and Council of Europe work.

Operationally, the minimum constraints should include: explicit, revocable, context-specific opt-in consent for non-medical cognitive inference; categorical bans on covert or compulsory cognitive-state inference in employment, education, consumer scoring, insurance, and most law-enforcement situations; strict purpose limitation and short retention rules for neural data and inferred mental-state data; rights to access, correct, delete, and contest both raw signals and downstream inferences; mandatory model validation for scientific validity and construct validity before deployment; independent audits and red-team testing for manipulation, discrimination, and sycophancy; and separate regulation of persuasion optimisation and relational design in AI companions, therapeutic chatbots, and always-on assistants. Special protections are warranted for children, patients, disabled users, service members, and other dependency-heavy contexts. 

Military and security contexts require even tighter discipline. Baseline monitoring for brain health may be justified for clinical duty-of-care, but the same infrastructure can drift into performance ranking, loyalty inference, stress policing, or coercive augmentation if law is not precise. Any military use should therefore be limited by necessity, proportionality, independent oversight, separation of medical from command authority, strict secondary-use bans, and enforceable rights against non-consensual cognitive manipulation. NATO and PLA materials show why this is urgent: the cognitive domain is already being framed strategically, regardless of whether neurotechnology itself remains imperfect. 

## Capability assessment, timelines, and monitoring priorities

**Established capabilities.** Machines can already decode some narrow intentions and decisions from brain activity in experimental settings; restore communication through invasive BCIs; classify fatigue, stress, anxiety, and cognitive load from wearables; passively model aspects of cognition and affect from longitudinal mobile and sensor data; and influence beliefs and attitudes through conversational and writing systems. These are real, operational interactions with the cognitive domain, even if they remain partial and probabilistic. 

**Emerging capabilities.** More portable MEG, improved neural decoding, better cross-subject transfer, contactless biomagnetic sensing, dynamic human digital twins, and multimodal passive monitoring are all moving from proof of concept towards ecosystem formation. The key convergence is between sensing, cloud infrastructure, AI inference, and long-horizon interaction.

**Research-grade but credible trajectories.** Over the next decade, the most credible trajectory is not full thought access but much better inference of cognitive state from mixed sources: speech, gaze, physiology, mobility, environment, context, and occasional neural data. In clinical and assistive settings, closed-loop neurotechnology and digital twins will likely become more personalised and adaptive. In civilian infrastructure, quantum sensing will probably improve weak-signal acquisition and tracking. In defence, cognitive monitoring, human–machine teaming, and resilience-oriented cognitive baselining are likely to expand. 

**Speculative but governance-relevant trajectories.** Over twenty to thirty years, governance should monitor harder forms of cognitive coupling: persistent assistants that become external memory and behavioural scaffolds; clinically integrated digital twins that predict changing cognitive states and intervene in real time; higher-fidelity non-invasive neural interfaces; and quantum-enhanced biosensing that increases passive observability of weak biological signals. These are not certainties, but they are credible enough that waiting for certainty would be imprudent. 
**Unsupported or pseudoscientific claims.** I found no credible evidence in the reviewed official and peer-reviewed sources for present-day AI telepathy, generic stand-off mind reading, operational use of non-local consciousness models, or reliable extraction of rich inner beliefs from ordinary consumer devices absent narrow task design and intensive modelling. Those claims should not be used as policy premises.

**Timeline analysis.** In roughly **five years**, expect wider deployment of passive cognitive-proxy monitoring, more clinical and consumer neurotech, stricter regulation of emotion inference, and more persuasive social AI. In **ten years**, expect broader deployment of adaptive neuroprosthetics, ambient cognitive modelling in health and work systems, and maturing quantum-sensing applications in medicine, navigation, and infrastructure. In **twenty years**, expect richer cross-modal digital twins, better closed-loop intervention, and more contested boundaries between assistance, optimisation, and manipulation. In **thirty years**, if current trajectories persist, the main governance issue may no longer be “can technology access the cognitive domain?” but “what kinds of cognitive access, modelling, and influence remain legitimate in a free society?”. 

**Governance recommendations.** The most defensible policy stance is anticipatory restraint: recognise cognitive-domain rights before normalisation; regulate inferred mental-state data as a special class; prohibit covert and compulsory cognitive inference outside narrowly justified medical and safety settings; require scientific validity, rights impact assessment, and independent auditing before deployment; restrict persuasion optimisation where dependence or asymmetry is high; and build international standards now rather than after path dependence locks these systems into workplaces, schools, defence systems, insurance markets, and everyday assistants.

**Open questions requiring ongoing monitoring.** The most important uncertainties are whether passive multimodal models generalise reliably outside curated datasets; whether non-invasive decoding can approach clinically or commercially meaningful semantic fidelity at scale; whether quantum sensing makes proximate biosignal capture cheap and ambient; how much persistent AI memory and relationship design amplify manipulation risk; and whether existing human-rights law can truly protect inferred mental content, not just raw neural data. Those questions are unresolved, but they are already governance-relevant.
